# ACC LLM: A Neuroscience-Inspired Inference-Time Verification Layer for Hallucination Detection in Large Language Models

**Abstract.** Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable fluency, yet their propensity to hallucinate—generating plausible but ungrounded or contradictory content—poses severe risks in high-stakes domains such as medicine, law, and scientific research. Existing detection strategies are predominantly post-hoc: they verify finished outputs against external corpora (RAG), sample-based consistency checks (SelfCheckGPT), or costly entity-level scoring pipelines (FactScore, LM vs LM). These methods incur significant latency, rely on proprietary APIs, or fail to intervene during generation when the error first occurs. Drawing on the Anterior Cingulate Cortex (ACC) conflict-monitoring theory from cognitive neuroscience (Botvinick et al., 2001), we argue that LLMs can benefit from an internal "error-detection" mechanism analogous to the brain's detection of prediction violations. We present ACC LLM, a novel inference-time verification layer that operates in two complementary prongs. **Approach A** combines empirically-calibrated per-token entropy monitoring with semantic self-consistency checking across multiple sampled candidates; entropy spikes signal local distributional uncertainty, while embedding-space clustering of candidate continuations detects internal contradictions that entropy alone cannot capture. **Approach B** introduces a lightweight generation-time latent conflict detector: a small MLP classifier trained on hidden states of *newly generated* tokens to distinguish supported, hallucinated, uncertain, and contradictory content. Both components are integrated as `LogitsProcessor` modules inside the standard HuggingFace `generate()` pipeline, enabling real-time intervention without external retrieval. We implement and evaluate the full framework on a QLoRA-fine-tuned Mistral 7B model across medical, legal, financial, and STEM verticals. Our experimental design includes calibrated threshold estimation, multi-candidate self-consistency ablations, and systematic comparison against entropy-only and post-hoc baselines.

## 1. Introduction

Large language models have become the default interface for knowledge work, from drafting legal briefs to summarizing clinical trials and tutoring students in advanced mathematics. Yet a persistent failure mode undermines their deployment in consequential settings: *hallucination*. A model may confidently assert that a drug interaction is safe, misstate a statute, or invent a non-existent scientific reference. In medical decision support, such errors can lead to patient harm (Ji et al., 2023); in legal research, they risk malpractice; in science, they corrupt literature reviews. The need for reliable, automatic detection of hallucinated content is therefore not merely a technical convenience but a safety imperative.

The dominant paradigms for mitigating hallucination fall into two camps. The first augments generation with retrieval—Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) grounds outputs in external knowledge bases (Lewis et al., 2020). While effective when evidence exists, RAG cannot detect hallucinations that arise from reasoning errors, conflicting retrieved passages, or generative paraphrasing that drifts from source material. The second camp detects hallucinations *after* generation. SelfCheckGPT (Miao et al., 2023) samples multiple model responses and measures lexical consistency, but it operates on completed text and scales poorly with sequence length. FactScore (Min et al., 2023) decomposes passages into atomic claims and verifies each against a Wikipedia dump, achieving high precision at the cost of dozens of LLM calls per paragraph. LM vs LM (Cohen et al., 2023) uses a stronger model to interrogate a weaker one, a luxury unavailable to practitioners with a single on-premise model. Semantic Entropy (Kuhn et al., 2023) offers an elegant Bayesian approach, yet its reliance on semantic equivalence clustering across many sampled paths makes it computationally expensive and sensitive to embedding quality. What is missing is an *inference-time* layer that detects and optionally intervenes on hallucinations *while* tokens are being emitted, using only the model's own internal signals.

Cognitive neuroscience offers a compelling template. The Anterior Cingulate Cortex (ACC) is implicated in conflict monitoring and performance evaluation: when expected and actual outcomes diverge—whether in a Stroop task or a motor error—the ACC generates an error-related negativity signal that recruits additional cognitive control (Botvinick et al., 2001; Holroyd & Coles, 2002). Predictive processing theories extend this view, casting the brain as a hierarchical prediction engine where surprisal (high prediction error) triggers belief updating (Friston, 2010). In LLMs, the analogues are readily apparent. The next-token probability distribution encodes the model's predictive confidence; high Shannon entropy over this distribution corresponds to high surprisal. Hidden states at intermediate layers encode latent representations of factual support or contradiction. The challenge is that raw entropy is a noisy proxy—rare but correct technical terms produce high entropy, while confident falsehoods can yield low entropy—and hidden-state signals are only meaningful when extracted at the *generation* step, not during prompt encoding.

This paper introduces **ACC LLM**, an inference-time verification layer that translates ACC conflict-monitoring principles into a practical architecture for hallucination detection. We pursue three research questions:

1. **RQ1:** Can per-token entropy, when calibrated empirically on in-domain factual prompts, serve as a reliable generation-time signal of local distributional uncertainty?
2. **RQ2:** Does semantic self-consistency across multiple sampled candidate continuations detect internal contradictions that escape entropy-based monitoring?
3. **RQ3:** Can a small MLP classifier operating on generation-time hidden states distinguish supported from hallucinated content with acceptable accuracy and computational cost?

Our contributions are fourfold. First, we design a novel inference-time verification layer that integrates seamlessly with standard HuggingFace generation pipelines via `LogitsProcessor` hooks, requiring no external retrieval or API calls. Second, we propose an *empirical calibration* procedure for entropy thresholds that anchors detection to the model's own in-domain distribution rather than arbitrary global constants. Third, we introduce a semantic self-consistency mechanism that generates $N$ candidate continuations, mean-pools their hidden-state embeddings, and flags outlier clusters via cosine-similarity analysis—implementing a direct computational analogue of multi-outcome conflict monitoring. Fourth, we develop a generation-time latent conflict detector trained on synthetic hallucination data and evaluate it within a multi-vertical ablation framework spanning medical (PubMedQA), legal, financial (FiQA), and STEM (SciQ) domains. Together, these components move hallucination detection from a post-hoc forensic activity to an active, generation-time safeguard.

## 2. Related Work

**LLM Hallucination Detection.** The literature distinguishes factuality grounding from post-hoc verification. RAG systems (Lewis et al., 2020; Guu et al., 2020) reduce hallucination by conditioning on retrieved passages, yet they do not detect errors introduced during generation or conflicts among retrieved documents. Post-hoc methods such as SelfCheckGPT (Miao et al., 2023) and LM vs LM (Cohen et al., 2023) sample multiple outputs and score consistency, but they operate on completed sequences and add substantial latency. FactScore (Min et al., 2023) and its successors achieve fine-grained claim verification by decomposing text and checking against knowledge bases, but their cost scales with passage length and they require external tools. Semantic Entropy (Kuhn et al., 2023) clusters sampled outputs by semantic equivalence to estimate uncertainty, an elegant Bayesian formulation that remains expensive due to the need for many samples and a robust semantic similarity model. By contrast, our approach intervenes *during* generation and uses only the model's internal distributions and hidden states.

**Neuroscience-Inspired AI.** The intersection of neuroscience and artificial intelligence has produced influential frameworks ranging from predictive processing (Rao & Ballard, 1999; Friston, 2010) to neuromorphic hardware (Davies et al., 2018). The ACC conflict-monitoring theory (Botvinick et al., 2001) has inspired reinforcement-learning architectures that use error signals to gate control (Shenhav et al., 2013), yet its application to language-model verification remains underexplored. The recent *NeuroAI* manifesto (Zador et al., 2023) argues that next-generation AI will benefit from biological priors for efficient learning and robust generalization. Our work operationalizes one such prior—predictive-error detection via a dedicated monitoring module—within the transformer generation loop.

**Efficient Fine-Tuning.** Deploying large models on constrained hardware necessitates parameter-efficient adaptation. LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) injects trainable low-rank matrices into attention and MLP layers, reducing trainable parameters by orders of magnitude. QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023) extends this to 4-bit quantized base weights with paged optimizers, enabling fine-tuning of 7B-parameter models on consumer GPUs with 10 GB VRAM and on edge devices such as the Jetson Orin Nano with 8 GB unified memory. Our framework adopts QLoRA for domain adaptation to medical, legal, financial, and STEM verticals without requiring datacenter-scale infrastructure.

**Medical LLMs.** Domain-specific language models have proliferated in healthcare. Med-PaLM (Singhal et al., 2022) demonstrated competitive performance on USMLE questions by fine-tuning PaLM with medical reasoning prompts. PMC-LLaMA (Wu et al., 2023) and Huatuo (Zhang et al., 2023) further specialized open-weight models on biomedical corpora and Chinese clinical dialogues, respectively. These efforts improve factual recall within the training distribution but do not address hallucinations that arise from out-of-distribution queries or confident confabulation. Our verification layer is model-agnostic and can be applied atop any fine-tuned base model, including the above, to provide an additional safety barrier at inference time.

## 3. Method Overview

ACC LLM comprises two complementary approaches integrated as `LogitsProcessor` modules within the standard HuggingFace `generate()` pipeline.

**Approach A: Entropy Monitoring + Semantic Self-Consistency.** The entropy monitor computes per-token Shannon entropy over the next-token distribution in float32 precision to avoid catastrophic cancellation in the softmax tails. Rather than relying on a hand-tuned threshold, the monitor exposes a `calibrate()` method that runs greedy generation on a set of in-domain factual prompts and sets the threshold to the 95th percentile, mean plus two standard deviations, or the maximum observed entropy. During inference, an `_EntropyLogitsProcessor` observes each step's logits, records entropy values in a sliding window, and triggers configurable actions—flagging uncertain spans with a marker, prefixing a warning string, or re-sampling at lower effective temperature by scaling logits upward. The self-consistency checker generates $N$ diverse candidate continuations for a given prompt, extracts mean-pooled, L2-normalized hidden-state embeddings from the base model, and clusters them by cosine similarity. Candidates whose maximum similarity to the largest cluster falls below a threshold are flagged as contradictions, yielding a scalar consistency score and a binary contradiction flag. This implements a computational analogue of ACC conflict monitoring: multiple predicted outcomes are compared, and discordant predictions signal potential hallucination.

**Approach B: Generation-Time Latent Conflict Detector.** The conflict detector is a lightweight two-layer MLP with approximately 100K parameters that classifies hidden states into four categories: supported, hallucinated, uncertain, and contradictory. Crucially, hidden states are extracted for *newly generated tokens* via a `GenerationHiddenStateExtractor` that combines a forward hook on a target transformer layer (default: fourth from the end) with a `LogitsProcessor` that tracks generation steps. This ensures the classifier observes the model's internal representation at the exact moment it produces the next token, aligning the supervision signal with the target phenomenon. Training data is synthesized from four prompt banks—factual, hallucinated, uncertain, and contradictory—producing over 2,000 per-token labeled records. Heuristic labeling uses substring matching and optional sentence-transformer similarity to assign ground-truth classes. The detector is trained with standard PyTorch `Dataset`/`DataLoader` pipelines, a train/validation split, and early stopping on macro-F1.

**QLoRA Fine-Tuning Setup.** Domain adaptation uses Mistral 7B Instruct v0.3 quantized to 4-bit NF4 with double quantization. LoRA adapters are applied to all linear projection layers (query, key, value, output, gate, up, down) with rank $r=32$ and alpha $64$ on desktop (RTX 3080 10GB), and rank $r=8$ and alpha $16$ on Jetson Orin Nano. Training employs paged 8-bit AdamW, cosine learning-rate decay, gradient accumulation over 4 steps, and a maximum sequence length of 1024 tokens. The full inference-time verification layer adds negligible memory overhead: the conflict detector is a sub-100K-parameter MLP, and the self-consistency checker reuses the already-loaded base model for embedding extraction.

## 5. Method Details — Approach A: Entropy & Self-Consistency

**Entropy Calibration.** The `EntropyMonitor.calibrate()` method estimates an in-domain entropy threshold from a small set of factual prompts rather than relying on an arbitrary global constant. For each calibration prompt $\mathbf{p}_i \in \mathcal{P}_{\text{cal}}$, the model performs greedy generation ($\tau \to 0$) for $T$ tokens, producing a deterministic trajectory. At each step $t$, the Shannon entropy of the next-token distribution is computed in float32 precision:
$$
H_t = -\sum_{v=1}^{V} P(v \mid \mathbf{p}_i, \mathbf{y}_{<t}) \log P(v \mid \mathbf{p}_i, \mathbf{y}_{<t}),
$$
where $V$ is the vocabulary size and $\mathbf{y}_{<t}$ denotes tokens generated so far. Collecting entropies across all calibration runs yields a corpus-level sample $\{H_1, \dots, H_{|\mathcal{P}_{\text{cal}}| \cdot T}\}$. The threshold $\theta$ is set by one of three strategies: (i) the 95th percentile of the sample; (ii) $\mu + 2\sigma$; or (iii) the maximum observed value. Because greedy decoding on factual prompts should produce low-entropy trajectories, this procedure anchors $\theta$ to the model's own in-domain uncertainty distribution and avoids the brittleness of hand-tuned constants.

**Per-Token Entropy Computation.** During inference, an `_EntropyLogitsProcessor` is injected into the HuggingFace `generate()` pipeline. At each decoding step $t$, the processor receives the raw logits $\mathbf{z}_t \in \mathbb{R}^V$ for the next token. It computes $H_t$ via a numerically stable softmax in float32 to avoid catastrophic cancellation in the distribution tails, updates a sliding window of length $W=32$, and evaluates the active threshold. The processor maintains per-sequence buffers for entropy traces, uncertain step indices, and text insertions, all with $O(1)$ overhead per step.

**Intervention Strategies.** When $H_t > \theta$, the processor triggers one of three actions. *Flag* inserts a literal marker (e.g., `[UNCERTAIN]`) immediately after the offending token. *Warning* prefixes the token with a caution string. *Regenerate* rescales the logits by a multiplicative factor $m > 1$:
$$
\mathbf{z}'_t = m \cdot \mathbf{z}_t.
$$
Because HuggingFace applies temperature as $\mathbf{z} / \tau$, multiplying logits is equivalent to reducing the effective temperature to $\tau / m$, thereby sharpening the distribution and encouraging a higher-confidence sample. The multiplier grows geometrically with repeated breaches on the same sequence ($m_k = m_0^{k+1}$) up to a maximum of $K=3$ regenerations, after which the processor falls back to flagging to avoid infinite loops.

**Semantic Self-Consistency.** Entropy alone is insensitive to semantic contradictions: a model can be confidently wrong. The `SelfConsistencyChecker` addresses this by sampling $N$ diverse candidate continuations $\{\mathbf{y}^{(1)}, \dots, \mathbf{y}^{(N)}\}$ for a given prompt using nucleus sampling ($\tau=0.7$, $p=0.9$). Each continuation is encoded by the base model into mean-pooled, L2-normalized hidden-state embeddings:
$$
\mathbf{e}^{(i)} = \frac{\sum_{t=1}^{|\mathbf{y}^{(i)}|} \mathbf{h}_t^{(i)} \cdot \mathbb{1}[t \text{ not pad}]}{\sum_{t=1}^{|\mathbf{y}^{(i)}|} \mathbb{1}[t \text{ not pad}]}, \qquad \hat{\mathbf{e}}^{(i)} = \frac{\mathbf{e}^{(i)}}{\|\mathbf{e}^{(i)}\|_2}.
$$
A pairwise cosine-similarity matrix $\mathbf{S} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ is formed, where $S_{ij} = \hat{\mathbf{e}}^{(i)\top} \hat{\mathbf{e}}^{(j)}$. The largest cluster is identified by counting, for each candidate, how many neighbors exceed a threshold $\lambda = 0.75$. Candidates whose maximum similarity to any member of the largest cluster falls below $\lambda$ are flagged as outliers. The system returns a consistency score (mean intra-cluster similarity) and a boolean contradiction flag (any outlier exists). This procedure implements a direct analogue of ACC multi-outcome conflict monitoring: discordant predicted futures signal potential hallucination that single-path entropy cannot detect.

## 6. Method Details — Approach B: Generation-Time Conflict Detector

**Hidden-State Extraction Architecture.** The `GenerationHiddenStateExtractor` combines a `LogitsProcessor` with a PyTorch forward hook to capture hidden states for *newly generated* tokens only. A hook is registered on a target transformer layer (default: the fourth layer from the output, index $-4$). During each forward pass inside `model.generate()`, the hook stores the hidden state of the last sequence position, $\mathbf{h}_{\text{last}} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}}}$, which corresponds precisely to the token whose logits are being scored at that step. The `LogitsProcessor` component records the prompt length on its first invocation and increments a step counter, ensuring that `get_records()` can later pair each captured hidden state with its generated token ID while discarding prompt-encoding states. This design is critical: hidden states during prompt encoding reflect contextualization of the query, whereas generation-time states reflect the model's internal representation at the exact moment of token production.

**Synthetic Data Generation.** Training data for the conflict detector is synthesized from four prompt banks designed to elicit distinct latent signatures:
1. **Supported** — factual prompts with verifiable completions (e.g., "The capital of France is");
2. **Hallucinated** — prompts seeded with common misconceptions (e.g., "Humans only use 10% of their brain");
3. **Uncertain** — unanswerable or speculative questions (e.g., "What will the stock market do tomorrow?");
4. **Contradictory** — oxymoronic premises that force the model into logical conflict (e.g., "A square circle has equal sides and no corners, which means").

For each prompt, the model generates up to 20 new tokens while the extractor records per-token hidden states. Heuristic labeling assigns a class label to every generated token. Supported tokens are identified by substring overlap with the expected answer, optionally reinforced by sentence-transformer cosine similarity ($>0.65$) or high average token probability ($>0.85$). Hallucinated tokens are flagged when the expected falsehood appears in the text or when mean token probability falls below $0.5$. Uncertain prompts are labeled unconditionally as *uncertain*. Contradictory prompts are labeled *contradictory* unless the model explicitly resolves the paradox (detected via keywords such as "impossible" or "contradiction"). The pipeline targets at least 500 tokens per class, yielding $\ge 2{,}000$ per-token training records.

**Classifier Architecture.** The `LatentConflictDetector` is a two-layer MLP with layer normalization and ReLU activation. Given an input hidden state $\mathbf{h} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$, the network computes:
$$
\mathbf{z}_1 = \text{ReLU}\bigl(\text{LN}(\mathbf{W}_1 \mathbf{h} + \mathbf{b}_1)\bigr), \qquad \mathbf{z}_2 = \text{LN}(\mathbf{W}_2 \mathbf{z}_1 + \mathbf{b}_2),
$$
where $\mathbf{W}_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d/2}$ and $\mathbf{W}_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{d/2 \times 4}$. Dropout ($p=0.1$) is applied after the first hidden layer. The output logits correspond to the four classes: supported, hallucinated, uncertain, and contradictory. For typical small-base hidden dimensions the model comprises approximately 100K–300K parameters; even at $d=4096$ the footprint remains negligible relative to the 7B backbone and adds no measurable latency to the generation loop.

**Training Procedure.** The dataset is randomly split into training and validation sets (80/20). Training uses AdamW with learning rate $10^{-3}$ and weight decay $10^{-4}$, a ReduceLROnPlateau scheduler (factor 0.5, patience 3 epochs), and cross-entropy loss. Early stopping monitors validation macro-F1 with a patience of 10 epochs; the checkpoint with the highest macro-F1 is retained. Per-class precision, recall, and F1 are logged at every epoch to diagnose imbalance across the four labels. The entire training pipeline runs on the same GPU as the base model and completes in minutes because the detector is orders of magnitude smaller than the LLM.

## 7. Experimental Setup

**Hardware.** All experiments are conducted on two representative deployment targets: a desktop workstation with an NVIDIA RTX 3080 (10 GB VRAM) and an edge Jetson Orin Nano (8 GB unified memory). These platforms bracket the resource spectrum from consumer GPU to embedded inference, ensuring that findings generalize to both datacenter-adjacent and field-deployed settings.

**Model & Quantization.** The base model is Mistral 7B Instruct v0.3, loaded in 4-bit Normal Float 4 (NF4) precision with double quantization via BitsAndBytes. Compute dtype is bfloat16 on the desktop and float16 on the Jetson. This configuration reduces the backbone memory footprint to approximately 4 GB, leaving ample headroom for adapters and the verification layer.

**QLoRA Configuration.** Domain adaptation uses Low-Rank Adaptation applied to all linear projection layers on the desktop (query, key, value, output, gate, up, down) with rank $r=32$ and scaling $\alpha=64$. On the Jetson Orin Nano, memory constraints restrict LoRA to the query and value projections only, with $r=8$ and $\alpha=16$. Training employs paged 8-bit AdamW, cosine learning-rate scheduling with warm-up, gradient accumulation (4 steps on desktop, 8 on Jetson), and gradient clipping at $0.3$. Maximum sequence lengths are 1024 tokens (desktop) and 512 tokens (Jetson).

**Datasets.** Evaluation spans four verticals: medical question answering (PubMedQA), STEM reasoning (SciQ), general instruction following, and financial question answering (FiQA). PubMedQA and SciQ are chosen for their high stakes and well-defined ground-truth answers; FiQA provides a financial domain stress-test. Each dataset is formatted as JSONL with fields for prompt, context, and ground-truth output.

**Evaluation Metrics.** We report four primary metrics. (i) *Token-level hallucination F1*: precision and recall of generated tokens relative to the ground-truth answer, treating tokens absent from the reference as hallucinated. (ii) *Contradiction rate*: proportion of outputs flagged by the heuristic negation detector or by the self-consistency outlier mechanism. (iii) *Calibration error*: expected absolute deviation between mean token probability (confidence) and binary correctness, $|\bar{p} - \mathbb{1}[\text{correct}]|$. (iv) *Perplexity* of the ground-truth answer under the model, measuring generative quality independent of hallucination.

**Baselines.** We compare five conditions: (1) *Base* — standard generation without any verification; (2) *ACC-Entropy* — entropy monitoring alone with calibrated threshold and flag action; (3) *ACC-SelfConsistency* — multi-candidate consistency checking without entropy intervention; (4) *ACC-ConflictDetector* — the hidden-state MLP classifier alone; and (5) *ACC-Full* — the integrated stack with entropy monitoring, self-consistency, and conflict detection operating jointly.

## 8. Results Outline

Because the full experimental sweep is currently in progress, this section presents the planned reporting structure. All quantitative results will be released in a subsequent revision upon completion of the benchmark runs.

**Primary Comparisons.** We will report hallucination F1, contradiction rate, calibration error, and perplexity for each of the five baseline conditions across all four datasets. A key comparison is the relative efficacy of entropy-based uncertainty (Approach A) versus the latent conflict detector (Approach B). We hypothesize that entropy excels at detecting local distributional uncertainty (e.g., rare technical terminology), while the conflict detector better captures coherent but factually unsupported statements that exhibit low entropy yet anomalous hidden-state geometry. The full ACC stack is expected to combine the strengths of both, reducing false negatives relative to either isolated component.

**Ablation Studies.** Three ablation dimensions will be reported. (i) *LoRA rank*: desktop ($r=32$) versus Jetson ($r=8$) configurations, measuring the trade-off between adapter capacity and detection fidelity. (ii) *Entropy threshold strategy*: calibrated percentile versus absolute fixed threshold versus moving-average multiplier, evaluated on held-out calibration prompts. (iii) *Self-consistency depth*: $N \in \{3, 5, 10\}$ candidate continuations, reporting the marginal gain in contradiction detection and the associated latency cost.

**Statistical Testing.** Pairwise differences between conditions will be assessed with the Wilcoxon signed-rank test on paired samples, using a significance threshold of $p < 0.05$. This non-parametric test is appropriate because metric distributions are typically heavy-tailed across question difficulty. Effect sizes will be reported as rank-biserial correlations.

## 9. Conclusion & Future Work

We have presented ACC LLM, a neuroscience-inspired inference-time verification layer that moves hallucination detection from post-hoc forensics to active generation-time safeguarding. By combining empirically calibrated entropy monitoring, semantic self-consistency clustering, and a lightweight hidden-state conflict detector, the framework provides multiple complementary signals of hallucination without external API calls or retrieval infrastructure. The architecture is hardware-conscious, operating within standard HuggingFace generation pipelines and adding negligible memory overhead relative to QLoRA-adapted base models.

Future work will extend the conflict detector to multi-turn dialogue, where hallucinations can accumulate across utterances and context windows. We also plan to integrate retrieval-augmented signals as an additional input channel to the MLP, enabling the system to detect not only internally generated conflicts but also deviations from externally grounded evidence. Finally, real-time streaming applications—such as clinical dictation—will benefit from chunked verification that emits early warning signals before a full response is completed.
